Information About Our Hour of Code Event

Event Overview

At 6:30 p.m. on November 7, Royal, Ayan, Aslan, and Nuray gathered all the neighborhood children and began the event. First, we set up computers and an internet connection, arranged a projector, and turned off the lights to create a focused atmosphere. Once everything was checked, Nuray introduced our team and explained our purpose. Ayan then presented slides about ADA while Royal recorded, and Aslan took photos of the presentation. Next, we opened the classic maze puzzle project from the Hour of Code event on the board. The children watched an introductory video, and Royal gave a brief explanation of the task. They eagerly began the exercises. Ayan and Nuray assisted with any technical issues, while Aslan and Royal offered tips to help the children complete each step more easily. Once everyone finished, Aslan asked for each child’s name, and Nuray wrote them on certificates. Finally, we handed out the certificates, and Aslan captured a photo of the children proudly holding them. The entire process took about an hour, and the event was a memorable experience for everyone involved.

What Went Well

As we were able to arrive at the course, we conducted our teaching sessions on time and had enough interactions with participants. All team members reported on time, and knowing their roles helped to avoid any difficult situations. Proper explanations were provided during the course, improving understanding and maintaining the audience’s interest. There was also adequate internet coverage among all the participants, thus making it easy to incorporate relevant web-based resources and interactive elements into the learning experience.

Main Challenges

The first major challenge was that the projector didn’t work as expected. After some adjustments, we tried to focus the projection only on the board. However, this significantly reduced the quality, so we had to expand the display area. As a result, not all the children could see the subtitles on the board. To solve this, we asked them to open subtitles on their own devices to follow along with the video. Moreover, a few students had devices on low battery and therefore required some time to look for suitable chargers or other kinds of devices. This not only delayed the course longer than was necessary but also had an adverse effect on their attention spans.

Preparation

Our team planned everything from the very beginning. First, we invited neighborhood children to join us. Then, we started searching for a suitable place to teach them. Nuray asked her teacher, and we finalized the date and time. However, we didn't have a projector, so we began looking to rent one. Fortunately, we found one and managed to arrange it. When we arrived, we set up the computers, checked the internet connections, and arranged the projector to create an engaging environment. We prepared an introductory presentation to explain our purpose and selected an interactive maze puzzle from Code.org (https://studio.code.org/hoc/1) to capture the children’s interest. We also printed certificates in advance, so each child would have something to commemorate their participation. These preparations helped us manage the event efficiently and make it enjoyable for everyone.

During the event, we inquired about their prior experiences with coding, and they responded that some of them were students at the Step Academy course, while the others were students at Code Academy. As we organized the event, we observed the passion in their eyes and their contentment with the event. At the conclusion of the event, we sought their feedback; did they enjoy it or not? All of them responded affirmatively, which brought us great joy.

In conclusion, this event was a memorable and enriching experience that contributed to the development of leadership, communication, and teaching skills. As Team 48, we were privileged to have the opportunity to organize the Hour of Code, under the guidance of professors, for children interested in learning about coding or pursuing their education at ADA University. The involvement of ADA students as mentors served as an inspiration for many of the young participants.

Acknowledgements

First of all, we would like to thank each team member for their dedication and support. Ayan did a great job introducing ADA to the students with slides, while Aslan recorded videos, took photos, and provided explanations to keep the children engaged. Nuray helped gather the children and introduced us and our purpose. Royal also supported the students by assisting them with tasks and providing basic information about the coding process. Each team member was actively involved in helping students when they faced technical or coding issues. Finally, we would like to thank Cavid Asadov for providing us with a place to complete our project. The combined efforts of everyone ensured that we could overcome challenges and create a positive experience for the children.
